The three rules

1 · The cap is a ceiling, not a target. Under is always fine. Over is never fine. It only moves down.

2 · The 20-minute delay. Every urge waits 20 minutes before it counts. Half the time the wave passes and you banked a hit.

3 · Every hit gets logged. Same machinery as your puff-limit system. What gets counted gets controlled.

These three are the behavioral core the research calls first-line — gradual reduction, urge-delay, and self-monitoring. No medication is approved for cannabis withdrawal; this is the backbone. Why they work →

What zero week feels like

First, the context that matters: the numbers below describe abrupt stops from heavy use. In pooled studies of over 20,000 regular users, about 47% get clinically meaningful withdrawal — but the THC-dosing trials show symptom load tracks the size of the drop, and you'll be stepping off from 2–3 hits, not ~30. You've done most of the receptor adjustment on the way down. Expect the blunted version of everything here.

First 24–48 hours: symptoms begin — restlessness, irritability, appetite dip. Cravings fire on old cue times; each wave passes in minutes.

Days 2–6: the peak, such as it is. The "why am I doing this" window. Clear it and the grade flattens.

Weeks 2–3: steady climb back to baseline. In long-term daily users symptoms can run the full 2–3 weeks or a little longer — give it the whole three.

None of it is medically dangerous. Worst case is bad-flu-week energy, and the taper exists to shave that down.

Sleep — the real project

Expect off sleep and vivid dreams for several weeks or more after zero — the research finds sleep disturbance outlasts every other symptom. The dreams are REM rebound: your brain rebooting dreaming after a decade of suppression. It's a progress signal, not a problem.

Sleep kit runs the whole tail: magnesium glycinate, consistent wind-down, phone out of reach. You've built this toolkit before. Sleep is also the symptom most likely to send people back — guarding it is relapse prevention.

A random bad night at week 4 or 5 means nothing. Don't read into single nights.

Structural supports

Your space: the plan works where you live — no relying on being somewhere else. The stash stays put during the taper; the cap is the guardrail, not scarcity. Then the night before zero, everything goes — flower, pieces, papers — so home isn't a loaded environment for zero week. Control in your own space is the version that lasts.

Friend is briefed: knows the zero date, won't enable a supply run, expects week one to be prickly.

Professional support knows — before zero, not after it gets hard.

Food: grabbable and easy, no forcing meals. Appetite returns around week two.

If you slip

A day over cap — or a slip after zero — is data, not doom. Go back to the last cap you held, hold it one extra day, keep stepping down.

In the research, lapses after years of daily use are the norm, not a personal failure — expected, planned for, and recoverable. The trend line matters, not any single day. Ten years in; the exit doesn't have to be flawless to be final.

Concrete rewards for clean days help too — that's contingency management, an evidence-backed add-on. Staying quit →

Backup & data

Everything lives on this device — nothing is sent anywhere. Copy a backup every few days and paste it into Notes, so a lost phone can't erase the record. The app also keeps an automatic snapshot from the first open of each day, and restores it by itself if the saved data ever turns up damaged.
